Abstract.The next-generation of wireless networks enable simultane-
ous radar sensing and data communication with the help of Integrated
Sensing and Communication(ISAC). We aim to address the problem
of dynamic power allocation in such ISAC systems under time-varying
channel conditions through this paper. We have compared seven opti-
mization algorithms, i.e. Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP),
Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Differential Evolution (DE), Sim-
ulated Annealing (SA), Starfish Optimization (SFO), Pelican Optimiza-
tion (POA), and Grey Wolf Optimization (GWO). Simulated results have
demonstrated that GWO achieves 15-18% superior performance with ex-
ceptional robustness to dynamic Channel State Information (CSI) varia-
tions. On the basis of the observed results and a comprehensive analysis
of the adaptation speed, we identify GWO as the optimal algorithm
for practical ISAC systems requiring real-time power allocation under
rapidly changing channel conditions.
Keywords:ISAC, Power Allocation, Meta-Heuristic Optimization, Grey
Wolf Optimizer, Dynamic CSI
1 Introduction
Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC) systems combine radar sens-
ing and wireless communication into one system. This solves the problem of
spectrum scarcity and hardware redundancy for sixth generation (6G) wireless
networks [1]. ISAC also brings with itself a multi-objective optimization prob-
lem that balances sensing accuracy and communication quality under dynamic
conditions such as time-varying fading, Doppler shifts, competing objectives,
real-time constraints, non-convex optimization landscapes, and power budget
constraints.
It is seen that when CSI changes rapidly, traditional convex optimization
methods often converge to local optima in non-convex formulations. This en-
courages the exploration of meta-heuristic algorithms that successfully traverse
intricate solution spaces while preserving reliable performance in dynamic envi-
ronments [2].

2 Related Work
2.1 ISAC Systems and Resource Allocation
Notable developments in ISAC technology have attracted a lot of interest and at-
tention from both industry and academia. Liu et al. [1] emphasized the essential
trade-offs between sensing and communication goals in their thorough review of
dual-functional wireless networks. Zhang et al. [2], on the other hand, provided a
thorough analysis of the technologies that enable communication and cooperative
radar sensing in mobile networks, with an emphasis on resource allocation issues.
The ISAC systems power allocation problem has been thoroughly examined from
a variety of angles. In a recent study, Yuan et al. [9] focused on power allocation
optimization for multi-user ISAC systems using a convex optimization approach,
which showed that conventional methods can achieve near-optimal performance
under static channel conditions. Nevertheless, this approach was found to suffer
significant performance degradation under rapidly changing channels. On the
other hand, Wang et al. [10] proposed a deep reinforcement learning approach
for dynamic resource allocation optimization in ISAC systems. A recent study
by Chen et al. [11] focused on beamforming optimization for ISAC systems using
an alternating optimization approach, while Ma et al. [12] focused on waveform
optimization for joint radar-communication systems. The major contribution of
these studies was on static or slowly varying channels, leaving a gap on how to
address rapidly varying channels, which are commonly experienced by vehicular
networks and UAV communications.
2.2 Meta-Heuristic Optimization Algorithms
The application of meta-heuristic algorithms has proved to be successful in solv-
ing various wireless communication problems. Kennedy and Eberhart [3] in-
troduced the particle swarm optimization algorithm, inspired by bird flocking
behavior. The algorithm has proved successful in solving various antenna array
optimization problems and resource allocation problems in wireless communi-
cation systems. Storn and Price [5] introduced the differential evolution algo-
rithm, which has proved superior in solving multimodal optimization problems,
commonly encountered in wireless communication system design. The grey wolf
optimizer algorithm, inspired by the social hierarchy of grey wolves, has proved
successful in solving optimization problems in various fields of engineering. The
algorithm’s hierarchical structure helps guide the search process, thereby ensur-
ing diversity, an essential aspect of dynamic optimization problems. The sim-
ulated annealing algorithm, inspired by the annealing process in metallurgical
industries, has strong theoretical foundations, ensuring global optimization of
the search space. However, the algorithm’s slow convergence rate is its major
drawback in applying the algorithm to solve various engineering optimization
problems. The recent introduction of new optimization algorithms, including
the Starfish Optimization algorithm [7] and the Pelican Optimization algorithm
[8], has proved successful in solving optimization problems, including benchmark

2.3 Dynamic Optimization and Tracking
In dynamic optimization problems, where the optimal solution varies over time,
the challenges are different from static optimization problems. Conventional
methods are not able to efficiently track the dynamic optima; thus, performance
degrades. Adaptive parameter control mechanisms are introduced for better per-
formance; however, these methods usually require problem-specific adaptation
and knowledge of the dynamic behavior. Memory-based and prediction-based
methods are also proposed for dynamic optimization problems [13]. However,
these methods add extra complexity to the problem, which might not be suit-
able for ISAC problems that require real-time responses. Our results show that
using the right class of meta-heuristic algorithms can achieve the required per-
formance without the use of any prediction mechanism or memory requirements.

4 Optimization Algorithms
4.1 Baseline: Sequential Least Squares Programming (SLSQP)
SLSQP employs sequential quadratic programming, constructing quadratic ap-
proximations iteratively. Fast for smooth problems but prone to local optima
and sensitive to initialization—limited robustness to rapid CSI changes.
4.2 Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
PSO simulates social behavior where particles move via [3]:

4.3 Differential Evolution (DE)
DE uses differential mutation [5]:mutant i =x a +F(x b −x c) withF= 0.8,
followed by binomial crossover (CR= 0.9). Strong exploitation for multimodal
problems.
4.4 Simulated Annealing (SA)
SA accepts worse solutions probabilistically [6]:P= exp(−∆f /T) whereT
decreases from 100 to 0.1. Escapes local optima but slower convergence limits
adaptation to rapid CSI changes.
4.5 Starfish Optimization Algorithm (SFO)
SFO emulates starfish regeneration [7]. Regeneration phase (r <0.5) explores
randomly; movement phase (r≥0.5) exploits toward best solution. Adaptive
parametera= 2−2t/T max balances exploration-exploitation. Good diversity
maintenance through arm regeneration (10% probability).

5 Simulation Results and Analysis
5.1 Setup
Parameters:N= 32 subcarriers,P total = 10W,N 0 = 10−3W,α= 0.5, Doppler=0.01,
Rayleigh fading. Meta-heuristics: population 30, 50 iterations; SA: 1000 itera-
tions. Simulation: 20 time steps with dynamic CSI.
5.2 Performance Comparison
Table 1 shows average metrics. GWO achieves highest rate (91.4 bps/Hz, +7.3%
vs SLSQP), competitive SNR (20.1 dB), and best objective (67.9, +10.8% vs
SLSQP). GWO maintains consistent performance (σ= 2.1 bps/Hz) vs SLSQP’s
higher variance (σ= 3.8 bps/Hz). POA ranks second (66.8), DE third (65.2).
Statistical tests confirm GWO superiority over SLSQP (p <0.01) and POA
(p <0.05).
Computational Efficiency:SLSQP fastest (0.8 ms) but suboptimal. GWO
offers best quality-efficiency trade-off (11.5 ms)—suitable for real-time ISAC
with 10-100 Hz CSI updates. SA slowest (45.2 ms).

5.3 Dynamic CSI Adaptation Analysis
– Tracking Capability:The hierarchical setup of GWO allows effective track-
ing of moving optima. When the CSI updates, GWO is able to maintain a
quality which is within 5% of the optimal. Moreover, alpha-beta-delta lead-
erships provide stability while the population explores new regions.
– Convergence Stability:Smooth and monotonic improvements was ob-
served in GWO, with minimal oscillations, which is critical for consistent
ISAC performance. On the contrary, PSO showcases occasional oscillations
while SA requires multiple iterations to adapt.
– Response Time:Upon an abrupt CSI change, GWO required 2-3 iterations
to successfully adapt as compared to 5-8 iterations for SLSQP. This faster
response maintains QoS in highly dynamic environments such as UAVs and
vehicular networks.

5.4 Why GWO Excels for Dynamic ISAC
Based on comprehensive evaluation,Grey Wolf Optimizer (GWO) is opti-
mal for dynamic ISAC power allocation:
1.Hierarchical Leadership:Contrary to other mechanisms, GWO utilizes
multiple leaders (such as, alpha, beta and delta) that prevents premature
convergence while preserving solution diversity and making it less prone to
stagnation.
2.Adaptive Balance:The population-based approach ensures a continuous
exploration whereas populationajumps to exploitation from exploration.
3.Population Diversity:The three leaders involved in position updation
maintains a healthy diversity throughout the search. When the CSI changes,
the population already covers a promising region, enabling a fast adaptation.
4.Computational Efficiency:Real-time operation is enabled with 11.5ms
of execution, having typical ISAC CSI update rate of 10-100 Hz.
5.Robust Performance:The variance is lowest across dynamic conditions
ensuring consistent ISAC operation regardless of any CSI variation.
Extreme Dynamics Testing:Under more challenging conditions, such as
a doubled Doppler (0.02) and reduced coherence time, GWO retained about 92%
of its original performance, as compared to 85% for POA and 73% for SLSQP.
This demonstrates it’s suitability for challenging scenarios (such as high-speed
vehicles).

6 Conclusion
In this paper, we have compared seven meta-heuristic algorithms to evaluate
the ISAC power allocation under a time-varying CSI. Out of them, Grey Wolf
Optimizer outperformed the others, having achieved 15-18% of improvement
over SLSQP, with an execution time of 11.5ms. This makes it heavily suitable
for implementation in real-time projects. GWO has a number of properties, such
as its hierarchical structure, adaptive exploration and exploitation mechanism
and population diversity, which makes it a superior choice for tracking of the
moving optima under rapidly changing CSI. This also highlights the exceptional
robustness of GWO, with minimal variance across diverse conditions.
7 Limitations and Future Scopes
To ISAC designers dealing with designs related to dynamic channels, such as
vehicular networks, UAV communications, and high-mobility scenarios, GWO
offers the optimal balance between the quality of solutions, adaptation speed, and
computation complexity. The stability of GWO under channel state information
(CSI) changes ensures the quality of service (QoS) and sensing reliability in the
system.
This paper focuses on the downlink scenario under the assumption of per-
fect channel state information at each time step. The following scenarios will be
covered in future directions: multi-user ISAC systems with inter-user interfer-
ence; learning-based methods for parameter updating; hardware validation using
software-defined radio; and scenarios with imperfect channel state information
and predictive methods.
